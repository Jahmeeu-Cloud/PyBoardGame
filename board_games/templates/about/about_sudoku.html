Sudoku, is a logic-based, combinatorial number-placement puzzle. The objective is to fill a 9 × 9 grid with digits so that each column, each row, and each of the nine 3 × 3 sub-grids that compose the grid contains all of the digits from 1 to 9. The puzzle setter provides a partially completed grid, which for a well-posed puzzle has a single solution.

The only rule that the game has is that the same single integer may not appear twice in the same row, column, or any of the nine 3 × 3 sub-regions of the 9 x 9 playing board.

The simplest strategy for Sudoku is the Only Choice Rule. This strategy takes advantage of the game rule to its most. As shown in the puzzle on the right, the B-a cell is the only cell out of the B row that isn't completed. Assuming that no mistake were made completing this row, the only number that can go in that cell is 4.

The Only Square Rule is used in a situation where a row, column or 3 x 3 cell has 7 out its 9 square filled. In this case, first determine what number is missing, and then see if any other of the same number controls any of the empty cells. In the puzzle shown on the right, the two green cells are missing number 1 and 3. However, there's already a 3 on row I, meaning that 3 has to go in the cell on row A

When there are more than one unallocated square but only one possibility exists for one of the squares, and the number must go there. This is the Single Possibilities Rule. In the puzzle shown on the right, looking at the purple square D-a, the only possibilities are 6, 7 and 9 in column a. But in row D there is already a 6 and 9 so that leaves 7 as the single possibility for square D-a. The single possibility rule can be used to solve all the puzzle squares highlighted in green.

The Two out of Three Rule can be used in situations where 2 out of 3 empty cells of either a row, column or a 3 x 3 cell are already controlled by numbers from other cells, which means that there is only one cell for the missing number to go. As shown in the puzzle on the right, the cell B-i, highlighted in blue is the only cell out of the 3 on column i where number 1 can go as all the other empty cells are already controlled by 1 on other cells.

The Sub-Group Exclusion Rule is the most complicated out of all the rules listed here. A sub-group is formed with any 2 or 3 adjacent cells within a 3 x 3 cell. Each color shown on the puzzle on the right represents a sub-group. Using sub-group, you can limit the number of location where a number can occur in a 3 x 3 cell which can help you determine where a number can go.

Important Tip: using the pencil feature to mark down where numbers can occur on a complicated puzzle can help clarify thoughts and see where certain number should go.

Although the 9 × 9 grid with 3 × 3 regions is by far the most common, many other variations exist. The World Puzzle Championship has featured a 6 × 6 grid with 2 × 3 regions and a 7 × 7 grid with six heptomino regions and a disjoint region. Larger grids are also possible. The Times offers a 12 × 12-grid "Dodeka Sudoku" with 12 regions of 4 × 3 squares. Dell Magazines regularly publishes 16 × 16 "Number Place Challenger" puzzles (using the numbers 1-16 or the letters A-P). Nikoli offers 25 × 25 "Sudoku the Giant" behemoths. A 100 × 100-grid puzzle dubbed Sudoku-zilla was published in 2010

The general problem of solving Sudoku puzzles on n2 × n2 grids of n×n blocks is known to be NP-complete. Many computer algorithms, such as backtracking (which is the algorithm used in this program to create and solve sudoku puzzles!) and dancing links can solve most 9 × 9 puzzles efficiently, but combinatorial explosion occurs as n increases, creating limits to the properties of Sudokus that can be constructed, analyzed, and solved as n increases.

Do you know: The number of classic 9 × 9 Sudoku solution grids is 6,670,903,752,021,072,936,960
